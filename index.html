My Passion

I knew I wanted to get into design when I was a small youngster. I would always play with LEGOs, and with my wildest imagination, I would always make the biggest pieces, most creative pieces. Not only had I enjoyed this very much as a hobby, but I also loved to draw art with my computer. When I first got my smartphone during junior high, it was the first time I had ever used mobile apps. I was extremely astonished at first. How beautiful these apps were appearing on just the palm of my hand! Facebook was the number one app I would always use, mainly because it was social media, and still being just a young teenager who greatly admired the aesthetics of a modern-day app built to connect with your friends, I could not resist the beauty of using this new technology for the first time. I knew I wanted to do something similar in terms of creating similar social apps some day when I grow older. With a continuous passion in digital design, I started to get into UI development and UX design when I finished high school and began my years in college. The very first application I had created was for a photo sharing website called Social Catch. Done independently for a web development course, I started this project out using a website called InVision, where I outlined all the significant details that should be included in the website, and of how all the photos should be arranged in an appealing manner. Within a few weeks, I eventually finished this website with a wonderful UI following the design I made for its appealing UX. When I became a part of more projects, I followed that same procedure: first create a visual design outline of the application using a source such as InVision, Figma, or Proto.io, and then proceeded on with the application independently or with a team. At a certain point, I was just loving my role with designing and then creating some of the most beautiful pieces of software. Whenever I put myself in the shoes of a user, I always think to myself, "Wow. I did that?" A truly happy and satisfying experience to witness.

My Team Experience

About a year after my graduation from college, I've begun freelance work as a UX designer working remotely with a sports advertising company called SportTechie. With a mission on linking its network to innovators and power brokers who work to shape the future of sports technology through storytelling and industry-leading events, I took on a role on assisting the team's goal, along with its management, by providing a new wireframe designed to attract more clients over to the company's website. Contributing to their success for approximately three months, I would not only work with improving the website's user experience, but I would also interact with developers and management through Zoom meetings, where we would plan our schedules ahead of time and make important decisions regarding how the web and mobile platforms should appeal more to the clients. We knew that keeping our clients up to date with the current news in sports technology maintaining the best UI possible was the only way to keep profits up. In this case, the team had worked rigorously on this goal. When it was the end of my time freelancing for SportTechie, the team had generously departed me with a sincere "Thank you for the time and effort, and we wish you the best of luck with your career" statement. Seeing this as a positive experience, I can say that this had definitely been an outstanding and thrilling thing to have been a part of that brought plenty of new ideas into my journey designing for UX.

QA Testing

During my time in college, I took a software engineering course for a class project working in a group of 6, where I took on the role as frontend lead for my team. When my team and I were developing our website for our project, we conducted QA testing to assure that our website was well-functional, and did not hold any bugs that would negatively affect the user's experience. Since we were to present our work for a local institution from Germany, we created a collection of our work, added documentation to it, and then checked to see if our application was meeting the requirement criteria of having a properly executable application. If there was anything wrong that we encountered, we would first leave the errors there, stop where we were currently at, and then "freeze" the project. Freezing the project meant that we would first stop ourselves from jumping into the next step, observe our work, and then record the information. As we were doing this, we would write down everything we had from this point and on, including all the errors we had from our current step. Doing so organized the stages of our project into "milestones," where we knew it to be an extremely convenient strategy that ensured the delivery of a top-notch, well-executable application in the end.
