For instance, for one of my projects called Charm, I had the desire for it to carry functionalities just like Instagram. In the app, people would be able to follow or unfollow each other, post photos from their phone's gallery, comment, share, look up registered users in the app, and have all of their photos displayed in their profile. Indeed, when it came to coding new exciting applications that involved posting new content for the connections people make on the app, it's the thing I'd taken the most interest in. Modern social media apps like Instagram have been most enjoyable to use, and it keeps me motivated to code similar pieces of work using my own creativity simply because it provides an enjoyable, everlasting experience with a great understanding of software development.

My Software Experience

While attending college, I was part of a semester-long project for a software engineering course where all students were divided into groups of six, each consisting of the following roles: a team lead, a front-end lead, a front-end member, a back-end lead, a back-end member, and a Github master. I chose to be the front-end lead for my team. Here, the most experience I've gained from this as front-end lead was guiding my front-end member through any trouble she had. If she, for instance, encountered difficulties when she was doing work assigned from me, I'd start a Zoom meeting with her, and essentially teach her some front-end concepts that would help her both resolve her issues and allow for her to use the knowledge for her own experience so that for the next time she encounters a similar problem, she'd understand how to do it correctly that time.

QA Testing

As my software team and I were developing our website for our software engineering course during college, we conducted QA testing to assure that our website was well-functional, and did not hold any bugs that would negatively affect the user's experience. Since we were to present our work for a local institution from Germany, we created a collection of our work, added documentation to it, and then checked to see if our application was meeting the requirements criteria of a properly executable application. If there was anything wrong that we encountered, for instance, we would first leave the errors there, stop where we were currently at, and then "freeze" the project, meaning that we would halt ourselves from jumping into the next step, observe and record our work, and then write down everything we had from that point and on, including all the errors that we had at our current step. Doing so had organized the stages of our project into "milestones," an extremely convenient strategy that had ensured the delivery of a top-notch, well-executable application.
